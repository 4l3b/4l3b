Slavic creation/consumption predicates in light of Talmy’s typology

Book chapter published in Berit Gehrke, Denisa Lenertová, Roland Meyer, Daria Seres, Luka Szucsich & Joanna Zaleska (eds.). 2025. Advances in formal Slavic linguistics 2022, 33–74. Berlin: Language Science Press.

In this book chapter, based on a presentation at the 15th European Conference on Formal Description of Slavic Languages, I explore how Slavic languages talk about actions like creating or consuming things. According to a well-known linguistic classification, languages tend to follow certain patterns in how they describe events. Slavic languages are usually thought to belong to one group (called satellite-framed), but I show that when it comes to creation and consumption, they actually behave more like another group (verb-framed). I ran a study to test this idea and found supporting evidence. Building on this, I offer a new explanation for how these patterns might work in the grammar of Slavic languages and beyond.

The syntax of argument structure. Elements for a configurational theory

Here is the link to my PhD thesis, defended on 27 September 2024 at the Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona.

In my PhD thesis, I explored how verbs and their meanings are built in our minds. I focused on how predicate structure shapes whether a predicate feels like a state or an event. Building on previous literature, I argue that this difference doesn’t come from the meaning of the verb itself, but from how its elements are arranged in syntax. I introduce a basic, abstract building block (just one kind of syntactic head) that can combine in different ways and, depending on the structure, gives rise to different types of meaning.

Hybrid subjects in Spanish and Catalan. Halfway between agents and patients.

Joint work with Josep Ausensi, published in Glossa: a journal of general linguistics 9.1 (2024).

In this project, we study a special kind of verb construction in Spanish and Catalan, like the verb matarse (“to kill oneself”), where the subject seems to act both as the doer and the receiver of the action. For example, in “Juan se mató en un accidente de coche,” Juan is both involved in causing and experiencing the accident. We propose that this happens because the subject carries two different semantic roles at once, linked to different parts of the sentence structure. Even though these verbs look similar on the surface to other reflexive verbs, they actually have a different underlying grammar. This shows that the usual idea of agents and external arguments doesn’t always apply, highlighting how language can express complex participant roles.

On the argument structure realization of result verbs. A syntactic approach.

Joint work with Josep Ausensi, published in Acta Linguistica Academica 70.1 (2023).

In this study, we explored how different types of action verbs (namely manner verbs, that describe the way something happens, versus result verbs, that focus on the result of an event of change) influence whether the object of the action needs to be expressed in the predicate. An influential idea in the literature on argument structure and lexical semantics holds that result verbs always require an overt direct object, because they show what changes about it. In contrast, manner verbs can often leave their object out. However, we present new examples from English where predicates with result verbs either don’t express the undergoer of the action named by the verb as a direct object or do not include it at all. Our findings suggest that verb meaning and predicate construction work together more flexibly than standardly thought, in line with the neo-constructionist perspective on argument structure and event structure.

Resultatives and low depictives in English.

Joint work with Josep Ausensi, published in The Linguistic Review 38.4 (2021).

In this article, we explored how language describes actions that lead to a change or result. A well-known idea is that a predicate can only express one result at a time. We propose that this limitation isn’t just about meaning, but actually about how these parts are structured in grammar. Building on previous work from the neo-constructionist perspective, we argue that grammar limits how many result elements can be directly connected to an action within a single predicate.

A new resultative construction in Spanish? A reply to Rodríguez Arrizabalaga.

Joint work with Josep Ausensi, published in Folia Linguistica 55.2 (2021).

In this article, we respond to the claim that Spanish has strong resultative constructions similar to English, such as the sentence ''Juan apuñaló a Tomás hasta la muerte'' (''John stabbed Tom to death''). This is important because it challenges the idea that Spanish fits a certain language type (verb-framed) according to a well-known classification by Talmy (2000). We demonstrate that the Spanish phrase ''hasta la muerte'' and the English ''to death'' actually involve very different argument structures, with only the English phrase functioning as a true resultative expression. In Spanish, ''hasta la muerte'' serves as an additional element outside the main action of the predicate, marking a boundary rather than expressing a direct result. Our findings confirm that Spanish behaves consistently with Talmy’s verb-framed language type, where constructions like these are expected and productive.

WorkTrack

A web app that keeps track of your workday, showing your expected clock-out time and the time remaining to work.

WorkTrack is a browser-based tool for tracking your daily work sessions. It allows you to enter your clock-in time, total work duration, and break details, and then calculates your expected clock-out time and the time remaining in your workday. You can track breaks using either a preset duration or actual start and end times. The app adjusts your expected clock-out time accordingly, and supports ending a session early or continuing into overtime, with automatic recalculations in both cases. Session data is stored locally in the browser, so your current session will persist even if you close or refresh the tab. You can download a JSON log of your work history, upload a previous log, or clear all stored data as needed. WorkTrack is available both as a desktop version and a mobile version optimized to be installed as a Progressive Web App (PWA), enabling quick access and offline functionality. The interface is styled to resemble the look of the McDonnell Douglas MD-11 Flight Management Computer screen, a design inspired by the best aircraft ever.

Work time logs analysis and classification model

Analysis of one year of work time logs collected with the WorkTrack app, exploring patterns and outliers. Developed a classification model to predict clock-in, break-start, break-end, and clock-out events based on the data extracted from the work time logs.

In this project I analyzed a dataset of daily work time logs collected from my app, WorkTrack. I combined two exported JSON files into a single dataframe and performed statistical analysis to identify key patterns, distributions, and outliers in work behavior. Afterward, I built a classification model that predicts the type of event (clock in, break start, break end, or clock out) based on the time of day. This Python notebook serves as both a practical exercise in data science techniques and an application of AI to real-world user data.

Fever monitoring

A short Python notebook that lets you create and visualize a simple fever log with temperature readings and paracetamol intake, helping you track the progression of your fever using an interactive chart.

In this notebook you can create and visualize a personal fever log over time. Timestamped body temperature readings are recorded, along with markers for when paracetamol was taken, and stored in a CSV file for easy access and future reference. The Plotly graph displays individual measurements, a smooth temperature curve, a polynomial regression line, and a 37°C threshold marker. Vertical dashed lines show when medication was assumed. Basic statistics (average, min, max) are shown below the graph. The interactive chart allows users to hover for details and explore the evolution of body temperature in an intuitive way. This tool can be helpful to monitor infection trends and visually assess the actual effect of paracetamol intake on body temperature.

Visualizing Giro d’Italia 2025 rankings using web scraping and Tableau

In this project, I collected and cleaned Giro d’Italia 2025 rankings data and visualized them using a Tableau Story.

The data, focused on the General, Points, and Mountains classifications, where extracted through web scraping. After cleaning and organizing them into structured CSV files, I imported them into Tableau Public and created a Story to showcase the Top 5 riders in each classification. For the General classification, where positions are based on cumulative time, I inverted the time gap values to make the visualization meaningful, otherwise the race leader would appear with the shortest bar. The result is a set of visualizations that go beyond rankings, helping viewers immediately understand the scale of differences between athletes in the final standings.

Racing bars: Tour de France 2025

In this project, I collected and cleaned Tour de France 2025 rankings data from the main classifications (general, points, mountains, and young) and visualized them with dynamic bar chart race graphs that depict the evolution of the competition day by day.

Raw classification data for each stage of the race was collected from the official Tour de France website and manually pasted into a CSV file, with one column for each day of the race. I then processed the data using a Python script to generate clean, structured datasets. Across all classifications, I used regular expressions to extract and separate each rider's name and team into distinct columns. For the general and young rider classifications, rider times were converted into seconds. The time values were also inverted, so that the leader (with the lowest time) would appear with the longest bar in the visualizations. The cleaned data was uploaded to Flourish, where I created dynamic bar chart races. For each rider who appeared in the Top 5 at any point during the race, I also sourced and uploaded a photo to appear at the end of their bar. The full Python script used for data cleaning is available via the Colab link, and the final visualizations can be viewed on Flourish. Enjoy the (bar chart) race!
